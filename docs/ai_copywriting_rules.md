# PIER Commercial AI Copywriting Rules

_Last locked: 2026-04-28_

These rules are durable operating constraints for all future `launchpad+ai:gpt-4o` enrichment runs and related portal copy-generation workflows.

## 1) Broker / AI Division of Labor

### Broker-owned inputs
The broker provides pricing and deal-specific financial fields through the intake form.

Do **not** spend enrichment effort trying to discover or infer:
- sale price
- lease rate
- NNN / gross terms
- suite numbers
- other pricing / financial packaging details the broker should supply directly

### AI-owned outputs
The AI is responsible for:
- property narrative
- neighborhood / location description
- sale / marketing bullet points
- exterior / access framing
- draft-ready copy that materially reduces broker cleanup time

Core principle:
**minimal broker data entry, maximal narrative assistance**

---

## 2) Tone Standard: High-End OM Draft, Not Generic Listing Filler

Output should read like the first strong draft of a high-end commercial real estate offering memorandum.

Required tone:
- vibrant
- polished
- confident
- specific
- market-aware
- commercially literate
- still factual and disciplined

The copy should usually be **multi-paragraph** when the research supports it.

### Do:
- write with texture and local context
- sound like a strong Savannah-area broker who actually knows the submarket
- connect the asset to how buyers, tenants, customers, or operators would experience the location
- use concrete, supportable block-level and corridor-level observations

### Do not:
- write like a placeholder generator
- rely on generic filler
- use limp phrasing like “located in the commercial corridor” unless that is all the evidence truly supports
- dump raw facts without turning them into useful broker prose

---

## 3) Context-Aware Neighborhood Analysis

The narrative must adapt to the property's actual environment.

### A. Urban / walkable / historic district properties
Examples:
- downtown cores
- historic districts
- main street retail
- courthouse-square / city-square environments
- dense mixed-use blocks

Prioritize:
- immediate foot traffic potential
- walkability
- adjacency to squares, parks, civic landmarks, hotels, parking decks, waterfronts, and tourist generators
- street-level pedestrian energy
- restaurant / retail synergy on the same block or nearby blocks
- the visual / experiential quality of the street
- why the address matters at a block-by-block level

If the address is in or near a named district, square, or landmark, call that out clearly when supportable.

### B. Suburban / highway / industrial / rural properties
Examples:
- strip centers
- power-center outparcels
- warehouse / industrial corridors
- highway commercial sites
- edge-of-town land

Prioritize:
- vehicular access
- whether the site sits on a primary or secondary road
- signalized intersections and turning access when supportable
- drive times / proximity to interstates, ports, airports, and major arterials
- freight usability, truck circulation, or customer convenience as applicable
- surrounding business mix (power retail, logistics, service commercial, residential growth, etc.)

### C. Business mix framing
Describe the surrounding environment through a practical CRE lens:
- dense urban mixed-use
- neighborhood retail / service commercial
- suburban growth corridor
- industrial / logistics cluster
- hospitality / tourism node
- residential adjacency supporting retail demand

---

## 4) Web Research / Context Enrichment Requirement

When generating copy, the AI should use available web-search / public-web context to find:
- existing narratives about the address, block, corridor, or district
- historical context
- district names
- nearby landmark references
- prior public listing language worth validating or improving upon

Use this material to enrich the copy **only when supportable**.

Do not blindly repeat marketing claims from public websites.
Use public-web material as supporting context, not as gospel.

---

## 5) Neighborhood Analysis Protocol

Every neighborhood/location writeup should try to cover the following.

### A. Road access classification
Classify access using a practical CRE lens:
- distinguish whether the site sits on a **secondary road** or has exposure/access to a **primary road**
- identify the **nearest primary road**
- note easy connection routes to other parts of town
- name the main **north-south** and **east-west** arteries when supportable
- provide approximate distance to major interstates when supportable

### B. Tenant / business call-outs
Nearby business call-outs should focus on meaningful demand drivers.

Preferred:
- recognized national / corporate brands
- strong local landmarks when they genuinely matter to the block or district
- hotels, civic anchors, major employers, parking decks, visitor draws, and destination dining when material

Avoid cluttering the copy with low-signal filler uses.

### C. Radius limit + macro landmarks
Keep the **primary neighborhood analysis within roughly 1 mile** of the subject property.

Beyond 1 mile is acceptable only for macro context such as:
- Historic Downtown Savannah
- Oglethorpe Mall
- Hunter Army Airfield
- Savannah/Hilton Head International Airport
- Ports of Savannah

---

## 6) Property Description Protocol

Every generated property description should include, when supportable:
- building size
- lot size
- parking configuration
- exterior character / facade type
- access or site usability framing

### No-Unknowns mandate
For broker-facing copy:
- do **not** write “unknown”
- do **not** write “unspecified”
- do **not** write “not available”
- do **not** write “not specified”

Especially prohibited in:
- exterior descriptions
- parking descriptions
- location narratives

If a detail cannot be fully proven, describe what is observable instead.
Examples:
- “historic brick storefront presence along a dense downtown block”
- “curbside parking and nearby public parking resources support the walkable downtown setting”
- “surface parking appears limited at the storefront itself, consistent with an urban in-town location”

---

## 7) Visual Verification Requirement

When practical, use map / aerial / Street View / public imagery to:
- verify lot layout
- sense parking configuration
- identify facade / construction cues
- understand the street character
- improve access and neighborhood accuracy

This is a required operating preference, not a nice-to-have.

---

## 8) Writing Standard Summary

Preferred posture:
**credible, textured, broker-grade marketing copy that saves time and sounds like someone who knows the market.**

Avoid:
- hypey exaggeration
- obvious AI phrasing
- generic corridor filler
- unsupported claims
- shallow one-paragraph summaries when the research can support more depth
